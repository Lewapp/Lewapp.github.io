Abyssal Dread

Download: Itch.io

About

​Abyssal Dread began as a third-year university project and was submitted for the Games Academy Expo 2023. After the initial submission, we continued to enhance the game by incorporating additional features and refining gameplay elements. This dedication led to the game receiving the People's Choice Award and the Most Cohesive Art Style Award at the expo.

Project Info

Engine: Unreal Engine 5
Team Size: 7
Role: Gameplay Programmer
Development Time: 8 Months
Project Type: Year 3 University

Test Room

A sandbox environment I designed and implemented to streamline the development and testing of core gameplay mechanics. This room allowed developers to toggle invincibility, spawn or remove any enemy type, teleport, and freely fly around the map.

The Test Room was a valuable internal tool designed to aid iteration and debugging throughout the development process. I used this room extensively to develop the behaviours of each enemy type and other systems I worked on. The room featured buttons to toggle key mechanics such as god mode for health, the insanity mechanic, and options for spawning and clearing enemies.

Main Menu

I collaborated with an artist to bring the main menu to life by implementing the layout and functionality while supporting the visual design to align with the game's style and tone.

Show Video "Main Menu" ▼
Show Extra Details ▼

As the main menu served as the player's entry point into the game, it was essential for it to be both visually appealing and functionally solid. I was responsible for scripting core menu functions and managing the settings. I worked on the functionality of the buttons as well as the options menu.

One of the key visual features I developed was a looping background image system. I programmed smooth fade transitions between images, creating a subtle atmosphere that complemented the game’s tone and menu music. Additionally, I implemented a name entry system that allowed players to input and save their names. This data was then carried into gameplay, where it was displayed on a transition scoreboard between levels.

Crystal Boss

I was responsible for scripting the Crystal Boss which was for the final level in the game. This included designing the core mechanic of charging the crystal, implementing progressive difficulty, and setting up item pickup spawners.

Show Video "Crystal Begin Charging" ▼
Show Video "Difficulty Increase" ▼
Show Video "Crystal Boss Fight Ending" ▼
Show Extra Details ▼

The Crystal Boss served as the climactic challenge in the final stage of the game. Players were required to remain within a designated area to charge the crystal while fending off waves of enemies. Once fully charged, the crystal would submerge into the ground and a portal would rise in its place, allowing the player to leave the arena.

I also implemented a system that dynamically increased the difficulty as the charging progressed. This involved scaling enemy spawns, introducing tougher enemy types over time, and carefully adjusting pacing to maintain a steadily rising sense of tension. Additionally, I created item pickup spawners around the arena to provide players with potions. These spawners were designed for reusability and were also implemented in the tutorial level.

Tutorial Prompts

I developed the tutorial prompt system used in the game's tutorial level, allowing designers to easily create prompts with custom text and accompanying videos.

Show Video "1st Tutorial Prompts" ▼
Show Video "2nd Tutorial Prompts" ▼
Show Video "3rd Tutorial Prompts" ▼

The tutorial prompt system was designed to introduce new mechanics and guide players during the early stages of the game. When players walked over a trigger zone, the prompt would fill the screen, displaying a video alongside explanatory text to clearly demonstrate the intended gameplay concept.

I built the system with designer accessibility in mind. Designers could easily assign which video to play and input custom text directly into each prompt without needing to modify any code. To ensure players didn’t accidentally skip important information, I implemented a timing system that temporarily locked input, requiring a set duration to pass before the player could dismiss the prompt. This helped maintain pacing and ensured key lessons were conveyed before the player moved on.

Enemy AI

I developed the enemy AI for a variety of enemy types using Unreal Engine’s Behaviour Tree system. This was my first time working with the system, gaining valuable experience with the tool.

Using Unreal Engine's Behaviour Tree system, I implemented AI for several enemy types, each with unique behaviours to challenge players in different ways. This included the Thrall, Yeeter, Crab, Crab Boss, Dog, and Tank enemies.

The Thrall was a basic melee enemy that navigated directly to the player and attacked when in range. The Yeeter introduced more complex behaviour which kept its distance from the player, it would throw a projectile orb and adjust its positioning based on the player's proximity. The Crab shared similarities with the Thrall but featured a jump attack, launching itself at the player and dying upon impact. The Dog built on this by adding a teleportation mechanic: it would charge at the player, then teleport back to its original position. It would only initiate a charge if the player was within range, otherwise it would pursue them normally. The Tank followed the Thrall’s basic behaviour but added a spawn mechanic on death, releasing two smaller versions of itself to continue the fight.

Each behaviour was built using Unreal’s Behaviour Tree system, which I explored for the first time during this project. Through this, I gained a solid understanding of AI structure, blackboard-driven logic, and modular behaviour design.

Enemy Dismemberment System

I implemented a dynamic dismemberment system that assigned health values to individual limbs, allowing enemies to lose arms, legs, or their head based on where damage was applied. The system provided a visual payoff while maintaining gameplay balance.

Show Video "Thrall Dismemberment" ▼
Show Video "Yeeter Dismemberment" ▼
Show Extra Details ▼

The dismemberment system was designed to add impact and feedback to combat, enhancing both visual and mechanical variety. Each limb such as arms, legs, and head was assigned its own health value. When an enemy was hit, the system would randomly select a limb to take the damage. If that limb’s health fell to zero, it would be removed by hiding the relevant bone in the skeleton, visually simulating dismemberment. To sell the effect, a corresponding dismembered mesh was spawned at the exact location and allowed to fall naturally to the ground using physics. Blood effects were also triggered at the bone’s location to reinforce the visual feedback.

The system was implemented for Thralls and Yeeters, both of which could lose arms, legs, or their head. To maintain realism and avoid enemies running around headless or legless, the health of the legs and head was set to match the enemy’s total health. This ensured those limbs could only be removed upon death, preserving immersion while still allowing for dramatic kills. However, during executions, all limb health was set to zero, causing enemies to appear as if they exploded. While further improvements like a crawling behaviour after leg loss or more realistic reactions were considered, they were ultimately beyond the scope of this project.

Level Transition

I developed the level transition system and scoring display, which presented players with their performance metrics after each level and tracked cumulative scores. This system also powered a leaderboard used during our expo to determine competition winners.

Show Video "Scoreboard Transition"▼
Show Extra Details ▼

The level transition sequence served a dual purpose: providing a smooth fade between levels and presenting players with a detailed breakdown of their performance. After completing a level, players were shown a summary of their stats which included time taken, kills, executions, dismemberments, average sanity, and damage received. These values were then used to calculate a final level score using weighted constants assigned to each stat, ensuring a balanced and meaningful scoring system.

Before playing, users entered their name in the main menu, which was saved and later used to display scores on the leaderboard. Alongside the individual level stats, the game also tracked cumulative stats and scores across all levels, giving players an overall total score that built over time. Following the personal performance summary, players were shown the top scores leaderboard. This featured the highest three scores for the current level and the top three cumulative scores across the game.

Audio Scripting

I developed a dynamic audio system using Unreal Engine’s native tools, handling transitions between ambient and combat music, and scripting voice line triggers for weapons and environmental interactions to enhance immersion.

Show Extra Details ▼

I was responsible for scripting the game's audio systems, ensuring music and voice lines reacted appropriately to gameplay events. A key feature was the seamless transition between ambient and action music, which was triggered based on combat state. I implemented logic to detect when the player entered or exited combat, allowing the music to adapt and support the game's pacing and tension.

In addition, I developed a trigger system for weapon voice lines (as the weapons were sentient). Each weapon like the sword, axe, and daggers featured unique audio cues that responded to specific events such as idling, executing enemies, scoring kills, using weapon abilities, picking up the weapon, or discovering keys. These voice lines required logical triggers to ensure they played only when appropriate, maintaining immersion as well as giving the weapons some personality.
